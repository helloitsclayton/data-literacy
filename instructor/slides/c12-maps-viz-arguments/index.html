We've looked at the HOLC Redlining maps from Mapping Inequality before, an argument is very foregrounded here (that red is bad and green/blue are good)
Similar map in appearance, but a less obvious argument. This is a Sanborn Fire Insurance Map from the Sanborn Map Company, published 1913 Some arguments:
  • Land is parceled out in the manner indicated
  • Private land ownership is a thing
  • Nothing but buildings matter (for fire insurance)
  • Footprint of buildings are as neat and tidy as depicted
This type of map might or might not be familiar. Clearly privileges highways (those signs are huge!) Do those lines (for roads or rivers) actually correspond to the width of the road or river? This is a rather famous image from the Belgian satirical newsletter Le Frondeur, depicting Belgium, Germany, and Russia splitting up a pumpkin representing the Congo. Underneath the pumpkin -- a map! Maps were essential in establishing and maintaining colonial control of Africa and Asia by Western European colonial powers. Essentially, lines are drawn on a map and then ownership can be assigned. A point made by Kidd in an earlier section of her article is that the way settlers mapped North America, only including natural features, erased any sense of indiginous use of the land and presented it as empty and available.
These are some of the land use maps that Kidd describes in her article on Inuit mapping, and were part of the case made for indiginous sovereignty over areas in Canada. Notably, this is from a report commissioned by a government agency, and as Kidd details it was motivated by the desire to get at oil in these territories.
