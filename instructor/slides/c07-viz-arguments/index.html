Data Visualizations as Arguments

These concepts come up in Data Feminism Chapter 3, which isn't due until next class, but I want to provide a bit of a foundation for this class's activities around data visualizations and arguments

September jobs report

The U.S. economy added 114,000 jobs in September.

The unemployment rate fell to 7.8%.

This is an example taken directly from Chapter 3 of Data Feminism, from The New York Times, and is meant to show how the same data visualization can be used to support multiple points of view with just a bit of tweaking. The source given is the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, and it is related to a NYT article titled "Drop in Jobless Figure Gives Jolt to Race for President" that ran in the Business section, under Economy

How a democrat might see things

There have been 31 consecutive months of job growth.

The rate has fallen more than 2 points since its recent peak.

Democrats might highlight the recent trend in job growth, focusing specifically on private-sector growth, and they might note the significant drop in unemployment since 2010

How a republican might see things

Job growth has been weak.

The rate was above 8 percent for 43 months.

Republicans wouldn't ignore the jobs deficit from before 2010, and might note the number of new jobs needed to keep pace with population growth (according to Paul Ryan, at least). They might also focus on the length of time that the unemployment rate spent above 8%.

There have been 31 consecutive months of job growth.

Job growth has been weak.

The accompanying text makes the argument much easier to pick out for both examples

The rate has fallen more than 2 points since its recent peak.

The rate was above 8 percent for 43 months.

Each visualization is based on the same underlying data, but different groups would use different presentations to make different arguments.

But what about the "neutral" version? What assumptions are being made here? The next two slides are larger versions of each graph that you can switch between as students think. Some examples that I came up with:
  • That number of jobs and unemployment rate are measurable quantities
  • That these statistics have been correctly calculated from the gathered data
  • That the gathered data accurately represents the true number of jobs or unemployment rate
  • That the observed number of jobs and unemployment rate for the years presented are related to a Democrat president
  • That number of jobs and unemployment rate are indicators of economic prosperity
  • That more jobs and low unemployment = good
  • The definition of "employment" used by the Bureau of Labor Statistics is the most relevant one
  • That time flows from left to right
