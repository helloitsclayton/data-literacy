What does "raw" mean?

Uncooked; unprocessed, unrefined.
In a natural state; not yet processed or worked.
From the Oxford English Dictionary Online

[Be sure to give time to respond] Ask students if they've heard data referred to as "raw" and perhaps ask what that means to them.
My argument is that data is "raw" in the same way that carrots are "raw" As you go through the different pictured examples of carrots, ask students if they'd agree with calling them "raw" They're definitely uncooked, but they definitely AREN'T in a natural state, not yet processed or worked Ask if students could describe what or how much processing the different types of "raw" carrots have gone through just by looking -- Probably some guesses, but it'd be difficult to say how many pairs of hands have touched each bit of carrot just by looking at the end result Finally, ask what they'd have to do in order to be certain

"Secondary" data

These carrots are standing in for "secondary" data, meaning data that's already been published in some form by someone else We can't tell say with certainty how "processed" these carrots are just by looking, and the same is true of existing data sets Just like with carrots, if we wanted to be sure of the process data have gone through, we'd need to be informed somehow (observation or documentation)

"Tertiary" data

Tertiary data refers to published data that is in more aggregate form, so it's only possible to see averages, counts, or other summarizations of the data Be sure to warn students away from using tertiary data for their data biographies

"Primary" data

"Primary" data is data that has been produced by a researcher for their own purposes A key focus of this course, and of a feminist examination of data, is that primary data isn't actually "raw" either Running with the carrot metaphor further, in order to harvest carrots they need to be grown, and in order to grow carrots you would need seeds Where did carrot seeds come from? Where did carrots come from?
Carrots were brought to North America by European colonists, probably the Dutch What we think of as the "modern" carrot was pretty widespread by ~ the 16th century (and, interesting aside, was much more common in other colors up until then) Upper right is Dutch c. 1630, bottom left is Spanish c. 1600, and bottom right is Dutch c. 1560s So carrots came here from Europe, but where did European carrots come from?
Our modern carrot in those paintings is the product of centuries of selective breeding and probably originated in ~ the 10th century in the area that's now Iran/Afghanistan/Pakistan That "selective breeding" began with wild carrots which had been cultivated in that same area for around 4,000 years

These are from a Roman copy of a 1st century CE Greek codex of medicinal plants and the results of selective breeding are already evident Top is what we'd now call a wild carrot, bottom left is the "domestic" carrot, and bottom right is their "wild" carrot; all are the same species, our wild and their wild are the same subspecies which wasn't recognized back then Even our word for "carrot" developed from ancient Greek to Latin to French and finally English
The point is that, just like carrots, data doesn't spring out of nowhere Data (primary, secondary, or tertiary) always results from a combination of historical and social contexts, even if they're really boring sometimes (like height in feet and inches) Everything operates within a specific social, historical, societal, political, etc. etc. context, and if we are trying to understand how systems of power and data interact, it's essential to recognize those contexts exist
